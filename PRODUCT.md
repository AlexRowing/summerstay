# Product

## Register

product

## Platform

web

## Users

College students on both sides of a summer housing handoff: those who need a place near campus for the summer (internships, research, summer classes) and those who need someone to take over their lease while they're away. They arrive with a specific job in mind — find a sublet in a given town, or list their own place and field interest — often on a laptop while planning the summer, sometimes on a phone. They are not power users of real-estate tools; they want to get in, find or post a place, and get out with confidence they can trust the other side.

## Product Purpose

SummerStay is a marketplace that connects students subletting their apartment for the summer with students who need one. It exists because the current options (group chats, Facebook groups, Craigslist) are noisy and hard to trust. Success is a student quickly finding a place near their campus, or posting their own and getting a real inquiry, without friction and without wondering whether the site is legitimate.

## Brand Personality

Friendly and approachable, grounded in trust. SummerStay should feel like a modern product built by students, for students: welcoming, warm, and easy to use, while polished enough that someone would trust it to find housing. The voice is plain and human, never corporate jargon and never gimmicky. Warmth comes from spacing, clear typography, tasteful color, and well-made components, not from playful decoration.

## Positioning

The simple, trustworthy way for students to sublet near campus for the summer. Every screen should reinforce that this is legitimate and low-friction, student-to-student.

## Anti-references

- **Cheap listings sites** (Craigslist, Facebook Marketplace): walls of unstyled text, no hierarchy, no trust.
- **Flashy consumer apps**: loud gradients, heavy animation, neon, gamified UI that competes for attention.
- **Generic AI-template look**: cream/beige backgrounds, tracked-uppercase eyebrows above every section, identical icon-heading-text card grids.
- Also not overly corporate: avoid cold, dense, gray enterprise-dashboard energy.

## Design Principles

Trust is earned through craft. Polish is not decoration here; clean spacing, legible type, and consistent components are what make a housing marketplace feel legitimate enough to use.

Warm, not corporate; clean, not gimmicky. The interface should feel human and student-made without reaching for playful gimmicks or flashy effects. Restraint reads as competence.

Let the listings lead. Photos and listing details are the product. The UI supports them and gets out of the way rather than competing for attention.

Earned familiarity over novelty. Use standard, predictable patterns for search, forms, auth, and navigation so students trust the interface instantly. Save any delight for small moments, not whole pages.

Honest over flashy. No overstated claims and no fake affordances; the interface should only promise what it actually does.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Hold to solid defaults: WCAG AA contrast for text, full keyboard navigation with a visible focus state on every interactive element, and a reduced-motion path for any animation.
